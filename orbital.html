Orbital resonance

This movie shows a blue planet orbiting a red star in a special way that is called 'spin-orbit resonance', where the spin of the planet is connected to the orbital period by a simple ratio. In this example, the planet spins exactly three times on its axis fo every two orbits of the star, which is exactly the configuration of the planet Mercury. For comparison, the Earth spins ~366.05 times per orbit (not ~365.25, due to the difference between Solar and Sidereal days), and is not in a resonant configuation. Tidal forces exerted by the sun on Mercurcy are thought to be responsible for driving it into this 3:2 spin-orbit resonance, much in the same way that tidal forces from Earth have driven the Moon into a 1:1 spin-orbit resonance, which is why you only ever sees one face of the moon.

It is thought that some eccentricity is required to drive planets into resonances such as 3:2, and in the example above the orbit of the planet was circular. In this movie this has changed to be mildly eccentric, with e = 0.15. The right-hand side of the movie shows the orbit, while the left-hand side shows the flux recieved and what the sun looks like from the point of view of an observer on the equator of the planet at the location of the large arrow, which denotes the 'up' direction from their point of view: the sun is directly overhead for the oberver when this arrow is pointing towards the sun and this is then mid-day. The two smaller arrows indicate the horizon and when one of these is pointing towards the sun it would be either sunrise or sunset for our observer. Note that the sun slows down in the sky around mid-day, which is a bit strange. A quick look at the previous movies, combined with a small amount of thinking, will convince you that one 'day' lasts two 'years' (orbital periods) for our observer, and that the system is exactly periodic over two orbital periods. Is this interesting? Maybe a bit.

Things get more interesting when we consider higher eccentricities for the same 3:2 system, such as in this movie where e = 0.25. The eccentricity means two things for our observer: First that the distance to the star changed appreciably over the orbit, hence the sun in the sky appears larger or smaller depending on the orbtial phase. Second the angular velocity changes significantly over the orbit, with it being fastest when the planet is cloesest to the star and vice versa. Note that the sun actually moves backwards in the sky here around mid-day! What the fuck, what is going on? Well, how the sun transitions across the sky for our observer depends on a competition between the (constant) angular spin of the planet and the (not constant) angular spin of the orbit. For these higher eccentricities the increased orbital angular speed around the point of orbital closest approach means that actually the sun moves backwards in the sky for a short time, prolonging the mid-day for our observer who can now get some serious tanning in. There is a critical eccentricity at which this can occur, and it can be found as a solution to some cubic equation; the critical value is e ~0.193. Mercurcy has an eccentricity of 0.205...!

Things get stranger if we consider our observer to be at a different longitude on the surface of the planet, such as in this movie. In this case the 'day' is acutally broken up into three distinct phases of daylight and over the course of one 'day' and the sun will rise thrice and set thrice throughout the 'day'. Pretty weird! In this example this manifests as one longer period of daylight when the planet is furthest from the star and then two short bursts of light with the sun low in the sky. This means that there are positions on the surface of Mercurcy when you would see this triple sunrise pheonomenan over the course of 'day', which on Mercurcy lasts for ~176 Earth days. Note that this also means that the total received flux, and the rate at which this flux is depositied, varies as a function of planetary longitude, as well as the standard latitudinal dependence. This is different to Earth, where all longitudes are (almost) equivalent in terms of the flux they receive over a year.

As the eccentricity becomes more extreme so does the weird behaviour. When e = 0.4, as in this example, the observer recieves more instantanoues flux from the short sunset-sunrise times than they do during the longer day, so the best tanning time occurs when the sun is low in the sky, but compensatingly close.

This may all seem like an academic exercise, but many planets have been discovered around low-mass stars, and the habitable zone of these stars is much closer in compared to that of our Sun. The relative importance of tidal forces increases more quickly than the gravitational force as you go near a star, it has an r-cubed dependance, rathern than r-squared, so planets in the habitable zone around low-mass stars are expected to be in spin-orbit coupled configurations. These could be 1:1, like the Moon-Earth resonance, but other situations such as 3:2, 2:1, 5:2, ... are also possible. So there could be funny aliens out there in the cosmos who have a very different conception of what a day means compared to that we have on earth. These aliens would also have to get used to the fact that the total received flux in one 'day' varies as a function of longitude.
